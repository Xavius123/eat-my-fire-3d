# Eat My Fire 3D — Lore & Campaign Design v1

> Design reference for narrative direction and campaign structure.
> Intended audience: development team. Not final copy.

---

## 1. Elevator Pitch

**Eat My Fire 3D** is a tactical squad roguelike set across fractured dimensions. A group of misfit warriors — each pulled from a different dying world — fight their way through unstable portals toward the source of all the chaos: the Convergence Core. Standing between them and it are two warring factions who have been fighting over dimensional energy for longer than recorded history.

Three ways to play. One truth at the end of every run.

---

## 2. Core Lore

### The Gates

Reality has cracks. Everywhere two dimensional layers press against each other, the membrane between them weakens. When enough energy concentrates in one place — a battle, a ritual, a machine core going critical — the membrane tears open into a **Gate**: a corridor between realms that stays open long enough for things to pass through.

Gates are not neutral. They are hungry. The energy that keeps them open is drawn from whatever is nearby — terrain, creatures, people. The more violence in a Gate zone, the deeper and more unstable the Gate becomes. This is why combat arenas get larger and more dangerous the further into a run you push. You are feeding the Gate with every fight.

All Gates branch from a single origin point — the **Convergence Core**. Nobody alive has reached it. Both factions have been trying for centuries.

### The Fold

The space between Gates is called **The Fold** — a lightless, pressureless void that connects every dimensional fracture. It is not survivable without training or luck. The player's party found each other here.

### The Two Factions

---

#### The Emberfaust Collective *(Tech Enemies)*

A technocratic empire that exhausted its home realm's natural resources two centuries ago and solved the problem through engineering. Their solution: **ember-core reactors** — machines that burn dimensional membrane as fuel.

Every Gate the Collective finds gets harvested. Their drones and sentinels are advance scouts. Their turrets are **Gate anchors** — devices that hold a fracture open long enough to extract energy. The Tech Overlord is a mobile command platform sent only when a Gate is strategically significant enough to protect personally.

The Collective is not cruel. They are efficient. They have run the numbers and concluded that the dimensional membrane is an acceptable cost. They do not consider the creatures living on either side of it.

**What they want from the Convergence Core:** Infinite fuel. One tap on the origin point powers their empire permanently.

**Their blind spot:** The Core is not a battery. Tapping it won't power their empire. It will collapse every Gate simultaneously — including the ones their civilization depends on to survive.

---

#### The Primordial Horde *(Fantasy Enemies)*

Not a civilization — a confederation. Goblins, undead, shamans, orcs, fire worms, and things older than names. They do not share a government or a language. What they share is a collective memory, passed through shamanic ritual, of what the world looked like before the Gates.

The Gates are wounds. The Horde protects them. Not with diplomacy — with teeth and fire and the bodies of anything that gets close.

Their shamans don't attack. They anchor. They perform rituals at Gate sites that slow destabilisation. The warriors buy them time. The bosses — the Goblin King, the Warlord, the Skeleton King, the Slime King — are not military commanders. They are **wardens**. Each one has held a specific Gate zone for generations.

**What they want from the Convergence Core:** To seal it. Permanently. Close the origin point and let every Gate collapse naturally over centuries rather than be torn open by the Collective.

**Their blind spot:** Sealing the Core doesn't heal the existing fractures. The Gates still close — but catastrophically fast. Every realm currently connected dies within a generation.

---

### The Portal Travelers *(The Player's Party)*

Four people with nothing in common except that a Gate opened under each of them and spat them out into The Fold.

They found each other in the dark. They kept each other alive. Now they move together — not because they trust each other, but because alone in The Fold is a death sentence.

They have a third agenda that neither faction knows about: reach the Convergence Core and understand it before either faction gets there. Whether to seal it, harvest it, or destroy it is a decision they haven't made yet.

| Hero | Origin Realm | Why They're Lost |
|---|---|---|
| Knight | Feudal realm, mid-siege | Gate tore open during a last stand; his army is gone |
| Mage | Arcane academy world | An experiment cracked dimensional membrane; she caused this |
| Ranger | Untamed wilderness realm | Followed something through a Gate; hasn't found it yet |
| Samurai | Realm being consumed by the Collective | Last survivor; came through hunting revenge |
| Ned | Frontier realm where magic met gunpowder | Chased a bounty through a Gate; has no idea how deep this goes |
| Death | Everywhere and nowhere | The Gates are breaking the natural order of dying — souls can't find the right afterlife. He has to fix it. He is not on anyone's side. |

**Death as a character note:** He is not heroic. He is not evil. He is a process that has been interrupted and is annoyed about it. Playing him means playing the oldest, most dangerous thing in any room — who just wants things to go back to normal.

---

### The Name — "Eat My Fire"

The Emberfaust Collective's identity is consuming dimensional energy. Eating fire.

When the Travelers fight their way through a Gate zone the Collective is harvesting, they are taking that energy back. The battle cry is a taunt and a statement: *you think you own fire? Come eat ours.*

It also works against the Horde: we are not afraid of your sacred sites, we are not leaving, and we are burning hotter than anything you have seen come through these Gates.

## 3. Campaign Structure

Three campaigns. One shared world, three different relationships to it.

---

### Campaign A — The Run *(Main Campaign)*

**Premise:** First contact. The Travelers find a Convergence Point and push through it. They don't know the factions yet. They don't know what's at the end. They fight whatever is in the way.

**Tone:** Discovery. Escalating tension. By the end the player understands the world and has a question: which faction, if either, is right?

**Map:** 10 columns. Standard pacing. Both factions appear randomly. Events and shops present throughout.

**Pacing:**
- Cols 0–3: Introduce fantasy and tech enemies. Small arenas. Light prop density.
- Col 4: First Miniboss. The Gate deepens noticeably — arena is larger, environment starts showing both factions' presence (Horde ritual marks on tech infrastructure or vice versa).
- Cols 5–7: Both factions escalate. Arenas get more complex. Enemy variety increases.
- Col 8: Elite. A named warden or a Collective commander — whichever faction appeared more in this run.
- Col 9: The Boss. Whoever reached this Convergence Point first is guarding the Core access. Could be either faction's boss, drawn from the pool.

**Depth levers:**
- Enemy composition shifts with depth — early runs feel manageable, late runs layer in shamans and turrets alongside standard enemies
- Arenas visually escalate through biome system (dungeon → forest → tech → merged/unstable)
- Events give fragments of faction lore without explaining everything

**End state:** The Travelers breach the boss fight, take the Core access point, and see The Fold beyond it. They don't go through. Not yet. That's Campaign C.

---

### Campaign B — Faction War *(Optional, Two Variants)*

**Premise:** The Travelers have been in The Fold long enough to make contact with one of the factions. Not allies — a temporary arrangement. The Horde warden they didn't kill pointed them somewhere. A Collective splinter unit offered resources in exchange for clearing a route. Now there's a relationship, fragile and conditional.

**Two playable sides. Two different stories. Neither is the heroic one.**

---

#### B1 — Ghost Protocol *(Collective Side)*

**Setup:** A Collective splinter unit — engineers who disagreed with the high command's plan for the Core — has been stranded in a Gate zone the Horde controls. They offer the Travelers ember-core fuel cells (translated: better mod rewards, more shops) in exchange for clearing a path through Horde territory.

**What you fight:** All combat nodes are Horde/fantasy enemies. Every arena has Horde iconography — ritual circles, bone totems, warden markings. The environment is dungeon and forest only.

**Narrative arc across three acts:**

*Act 1 (cols 0–3):* Clearing outer Horde patrols. The Collective contact is grateful and informative. They explain ember-core tech from their perspective — it's clean, it's necessary, the membrane would collapse anyway. The Travelers are skeptical but paid.

*Act 2 (cols 4–7):* A Miniboss — a Horde Warden who has held this Gate for sixty years. After the fight, the Travelers find the warden's ritual site. The Mage or Ranger recognises something: the ritual wasn't aggression. It was maintenance. The Gate they just fought through was being *stabilised* by the warden. Without the ritual, it will destabilise faster.

The Collective contact's response: "That's acceptable. We'll be finished extracting before it collapses." The Travelers are now paying attention differently.

*Act 3 (cols 8–9):* The Collective contact's team reaches their extraction site — which is not a fuel cache. It's a Gate anchor array pointed at a populated realm. The Elite fight is Collective security turning on the Travelers who now know too much. The Final Boss is the Collective field commander.

**What the player learns:** The splinter unit wasn't dissident. They were the advance team. The Collective's plan for this Convergence Point was never fuel. It was a controlled collapse of a realm they want the land of.

**Mechanical depth:**
- All rewards are tech-flavored (Collective surplus: higher charge weapons, ranged mods, armor with damage reduction)
- No random faction nodes — enemy pool is 100% Horde, which means a player can min-max against one enemy type across the whole run
- Mid-run revelation forces a choice at the Act 2 miniboss: take the Collective's bonus reward for clearing the warden, or refuse it (losing gold but keeping a moral ledger the game tracks quietly)
- The final boss fight is harder if you took every Collective reward — they anticipated you'd be well-equipped and scaled accordingly
- Unlocks: completing Ghost Protocol unlocks Ned for the main roster (he was working a Collective contract when he got pulled through)

---

#### B2 — Warden's Path *(Horde Side)*

**Setup:** The Travelers stumbled into a Horde-sacred Gate zone and instead of fighting their way out, one of them (implied: Death or the Ranger) performed a gesture the Horde recognised. A shaman made contact. The arrangement: follow the Horde's warden network through Collective-held territory to reach a deep Gate the Horde has been unable to reach for decades. In exchange, the Travelers get passage and the Horde's ritual knowledge (translated: different mod rewards, HP restoration events).

**What you fight:** All combat nodes are Collective/tech enemies. Arenas are industrial and tech-biome throughout. The Horde are present but as background — shamans observing the Travelers' fights from a distance, performing maintenance on Gates the Travelers clear.

**Narrative arc across three acts:**

*Act 1 (cols 0–3):* Pushing through Collective outposts. The shaman guide communicates through gestures and ritual objects — no shared language. The Travelers are watched but not helped.

*Act 2 (cols 4–7):* The Miniboss is a Collective Tech Commander who has been systematically destroying Horde ritual sites to destabilise Gates deliberately — making them collapse faster and releasing a burst of extractable energy. After the fight, the shaman performs a ritual over the ruined site. It's grief. The Travelers understand now that the Horde have been watching their sacred sites die one by one.

*Act 3 (cols 8–9):* The deep Gate the Horde wants to reach — the reason for this arrangement — turns out to be where the shaman's people crossed over generations ago. They are trying to go home. The Collective has a full extraction array guarding it. The Elite is a Collective siege unit. The Final Boss is the extraction array's automated defense system — a machine so old it predates the current Collective command, still running a centuries-old harvest protocol.

**What the player learns:** The Horde isn't protecting the Gates for mystical reasons. They are trapped on the wrong side of them. The Collective's harvesting has been systematically destroying any Gate large enough for mass passage. The Horde's aggression is desperation.

**Mechanical depth:**
- All rewards are Horde-flavored (ritual mods: HP restoration, status effect resistance, melee damage amplifiers)
- Enemy pool is 100% Collective — players who understand tech enemy patterns (immobile turrets, sentinel positioning) have a strong advantage
- HP restoration events appear as shaman blessings — frequency tied to how many Collective Gate anchors the Travelers have destroyed (rewarding aggression over passive play)
- The final boss fight (automated defense system) does not respond to morale or negotiation — it is the only truly neutral enemy in any campaign, which makes it feel different
- Unlocks: completing Warden's Path unlocks Death for the main roster (the shaman recognised him and extended the arrangement specifically because of who he is)

---

### Campaign C — Ironclad *(Optional, Prestige Campaign)*

**Premise:** The Convergence Core. The real one. The Travelers have been deep enough now to know where it is and what's waiting there. Both factions have committed full forces. The Gate is destabilising faster than either faction predicted. And something in The Fold that is not the Horde, not the Collective, and not any of the Travelers has started moving.

**Tone:** Dread. Escalation. The feeling that the world is ending and you are the only thing between it and the thing causing it.

**Prerequisites:** Completing The Run unlocks Ironclad. Completing both Faction War variants provides lore context but is not required.

**Map:** 14 columns. Three acts with explicit chapter breaks. No permanent between-fight HP restoration except at chapter breaks.

---

**Structure:**

*Chapter 1 — The Approach (cols 0–4)*
Both factions are present. Mixed enemy pools from the start — Horde patrols and Collective drones in the same arena. They are not fighting each other. They have a ceasefire. That alone tells the Travelers something is wrong.

Chapter 1 ends with **Miniboss 1**: a joint Horde-Collective checkpoint — a Collective commander and a Horde warden fighting side by side. The ceasefire is real. Whatever is ahead frightened both of them into it.

*Chapter Break after col 4:*
A forced event node. The shaman from Warden's Path (if completed) or a new one appears. Through gesture and ritual object, the Travelers are shown a vision: The Fold is not empty anymore. Something has been growing in the space between Gates since the first one ever opened, feeding on leaked dimensional energy. The Collective's harvesting didn't invent it — but it accelerated it.

It has a name in the Horde's oldest memory: **The Ungate**. Not a creature. Not a faction. A process — the membrane's immune response to being torn open too many times. It is trying to close every Gate permanently from the inside, consuming everything in The Fold to do it.

HP is fully restored at the chapter break. This is the only time.

*Chapter 2 — The Convergence (cols 5–9)*
The enemy pool shifts. Still mixed Horde and Collective, but now some arenas have a third element: **Ungate Fragments** — enemies that don't belong to either faction. Visually and mechanically they should feel wrong. No lore explanation yet. Just encounters that break the pattern.

Chapter 2 ends with **Miniboss 2**: an Ungate Fragment boss — the first coherent piece of The Ungate to achieve something like intent. It is not using tactics learned from the factions. It is using tactics learned from watching the Travelers across every run.

*Chapter 3 — The Core (cols 10–13)*
The arena environments break down. Both biomes appear simultaneously in the same arena — dungeon stonework crumbling into tech infrastructure crumbling into something that looks like neither. The Ungate is consuming the Convergence Point from the inside.

Col 12: **Elite** — The final Collective commander and the final Horde warden, both waiting. Not to fight the Travelers. To ask them to stop. Both factions have realised that reaching the Core will feed The Ungate enough energy to finish the job. Sealing it, harvesting it, destroying it — all of it makes it stronger. They want the Travelers to turn back.

The Travelers don't turn back.

Col 13: **The Final Boss — The Ungate**

Not a creature. A state of reality. It has taken on a shape assembled from fragments of everything it has consumed — Collective machine parts, Horde bone and ritual object, and pieces of The Fold itself. It fights differently each run because it has learned from every previous run. It does not have phases. It has adaptations.

Mechanically: The Ungate reads the player's dominant strategy across the run and introduces a counter mid-fight. A player who stacked ranged attacks finds the arena shrinking. A player who relied on one high-damage unit finds that unit targeted with a debuff that inverts attack into self-damage. It is not unfair — it telegraphs what it's doing. But it forces adaptation in the final fight of the hardest campaign.

**End state:** The Travelers don't seal the Core. They don't harvest it. They collapse The Fold access point from inside — sealing themselves away from every realm they came from. The Core is isolated. The Ungate has nothing left to feed on. It recedes.

The final screen: The Fold. Four characters. No way home. The question of whether that's a victory or a tragedy is left to the player.

---

**Ironclad Mechanical Depth:**

| System | How It Works |
|---|---|
| No HP restore | Partyup HP persists across all fights. Chapter break is the only restore. |
| Mixed enemy pools | From col 0, both factions appear in the same arena. Standard enemy AI unchanged. |
| Ungate Fragments | Third enemy type. Fewer in numbers, much higher individual threat. No faction tag — different reward rules. |
| Adaptive final boss | Tracks player's most-used attack type across the run. Counters it in the final fight. |
| No shops | Shop nodes replaced with event nodes. Rewards are event-driven, not purchased. |
| Longer arenas | Boss arena: 16×14. Environment renderer shows both biomes simultaneously. |
| Death's unique rule | Death cannot be killed. He is reduced to 1 HP and can no longer act when he would die. He can be restored to full HP by standing adjacent to an ally who uses a healing action. This is the only run where this matters mechanically because attrition is real. |

## 4. What Makes The Optional Campaigns Deeper

The main run teaches the game. The optional campaigns recontextualise it.

| Layer | The Run | Faction War | Ironclad |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Enemy knowledge** | Both factions, randomised | One faction only — deep mastery possible | Both factions plus a third that breaks the rules |
| **Moral position** | Neutral — just surviving | Complicit — you chose a side | Responsible — you know more than both factions |
| **Narrative payoff** | World introduction | A specific story with a twist | The actual ending |
| **Mechanical pressure** | Standard | Locked pool enables min-maxing | No HP restore, adaptive boss |
| **Reward structure** | Mixed mods | Faction-flavored mods | Event-only rewards, no shop |
| **Roster unlock** | — | Ned (Ghost Protocol), Death (Warden's Path) | — |
| **Length** | ~35 min | ~35 min | ~75 min |

The optional campaigns are deeper because they are not harder versions of the same experience. They are different questions:

- **Ghost Protocol** asks: *what does it cost to be useful to someone with bad intentions?*
- **Warden's Path** asks: *what do you owe to people whose cause you didn't know you were fighting?*
- **Ironclad** asks: *if stopping something catastrophic costs you everything, is it still worth it?*

The Run never asks those questions. It just asks: *can you survive?*

## 5. Open Design Questions

1. **The Fold as a scene** — Should there be a persistent between-run space where lore accumulates? A campfire equivalent where characters talk between campaigns?
2. **Faction War unlock order** — Does completing Ghost Protocol first change anything about Warden's Path? Should the mid-run revelation in one change a detail in the other?
3. **The Ungate's visual identity** — Needs a distinct aesthetic that isn't just "both biomes mixed." What does a dimensional immune response look like?
4. **Ned and Death as unlocks** — If they're unlocked through Faction War, are they available in The Run on subsequent playthroughs? Or do they only appear in specific campaigns?
5. **The ending of Ironclad** — Is the final screen a literal image, a text card, or something interactive? The ambiguity of the ending only works if the presentation earns it.
